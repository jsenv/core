S-curve tab corners

Three techniques to achieve the "binder" S-curve at the bottom corners of a selected tab — a convex arc at the top, a concave arc at the bottom where the tab meets the panel border. No pseudo-elements used.

1 — CSS mask (radial-gradient punched corners)

Four mask layers: two radial-gradient circles punch concave arcs at the bottom corners; two linear-gradient fills cover the rest of the tab. Limitation: the border stroke does not follow the arc — it is just hidden by the mask. Works cleanly when the tab background matches the page background.

2 — CSS clip-path: polygon() (chamfered, no curves)

polygon() only supports straight-line vertices — no bezier curves. The result is chamfered (diagonal) corners, not arcs. Shown here to illustrate the limitation; the S-curve is not achievable with polygon alone. path() inside clip-path supports beziers but uses absolute pixel coordinates and does not scale with the element.

3 — Inline SVG per tab (true bezier S-curve)

Each tab has an absolutely-positioned SVG that draws the full shape. Quadratic beziers: convex arcs at the top corners (control point inside the tab), concave arcs at the bottom corners (control point pushed below the tab baseline, creating the inward S-curve). Fill and stroke both follow the exact geometry. Best result — the stroke traces the true S-curve. The active tab erases its bottom stroke line to seamlessly connect with the panel border.
